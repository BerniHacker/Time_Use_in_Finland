Time Use in Finland¶

by Bernardo Di Chiara

November 6th 2018

This project consists in formulating a set of research questions and then searching and importing two data sets from Internet that could help to answer those questions.

The data sets are cleansed and manipulated by using Pandas library.

Then, some exploratory data analysis is performed and plots are created by using Matplotlib library. When creating those plots, graphical heuristics like Edward Tufte data-ink ratio and Alberto Cairo's principles are followed in order to produce visualizations that are truthful, functional, insightful and pleasant to the eye.

The data manipulation process is done by Python programming as much as possible.

1. Research Questions¶

Based on information released by Eurostat (the European statistical institution), Finns are quite satisfied about the way they use their time [1]. On the other hand, Germans are among the less satisfied ones.

This paper focus on the way Finns use their time, on the differences between men and women and on the changes of those figures over time. Comparison with data related to Germany is done.

The questions that we are trying to answer are:

  • How much time Finns spend on average working? How much on free time?
  • What are the differences between men and women? How those values have been changing over time?
  • How those values differ from values related to Germany?

2. Used Data¶

The two data sets used in this project are part of the harmonised European time use survey carried out in several European countries, whose scope is the resident population of the country living in private households ('). The data has been collected through an interview survey by using randomly selected samples. Samples' sizes have been defined in order to be representative of the population in the respective countries (over 5000 people have been included for Finland which has a population over 5 millions whereas for Germany, which has a population over 70.000, a sample of over 12000 people has been defined).

Respondents have been asked to keep an accurate diary of their time use over a two-days period. The diary period has been randomly designated and defined so to cover a full 12 months consecutive period. The survey examines working time, the time used on domestic work, sleeping and eating, leisure time activities and the time spent with other people as well as the place of activity. In addition, the survey also studies the rhythms of time use according to day, day of the week and season.

The activities described by the respondents themselves are coded according to a 146-category classification of work activities. Background variables for the time use data are such as the main type of activity, age, stage in life, level of education, socio-economic group, type of municipality, major region, province, time of the year, day of the week.

The first used data set [2] is publicly available from Statistics Finland (Tilastokeskus), the national statistical institution in Finland.

It contains information about how people use their time, differentiated by gender, according to four different surveys run at a distance of approximately 10 years (starting in 1979, 1987, 1999 and 2009). In this data set, day activities have been divided in 26 different categories.

The second data set [3] is available from Eurostat, the European statistical institution. It contains similar data from other nine European countries. The data contained in tables 1.1 and 1.2 is used in this project and in particular the columns related to Germany. Data related to this Country has been collected in years 2001-2002.

Additional information about those surveys can be found in the document "Comparable time use statistics. National tables from 10 European countries" [4]

(') European guidelines for such surveys [[5]](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/documents/3859598/5909673/KS-RA-08-014-EN.PDF/a745ca2e-7dc6-48a9-a36c-000ad120380e?version=1.0) can be found at the link visible in the References section.

The plot above shows the average time (in percentage of the time of a day) used for the total population (Finnish man and women living in private households) in year 1999/2000 for different grouped categories of activities (blue bars). It also shows similar data for Germans (green bars) and the differences of Finnish data and German data in percentage of Finnish data (red bars).

On average Finns spent over 22.64% of the time for free activities and 19.24% of the time for work and study related activities. Those figures are the estimated averages of the all population.

The largest differences with data from Germany in percentage compared to Finnish data is the time spent for non-work-related travel that is 3.82 for Finns and 5.73% for Germans (50% more). The next big difference is the time spent for work related activities that is 19.24% for Finns and only 11.8 for Germans (38.67% less). Finns spend also 31.69% less time in personal care (8.33%) compared to Germans (10.97%).

So the quite counter-intuitive result is that there is an association between the group that states to be more satisfied with the usage of their time and the group that works more, travels less for leisure or domestic work and spends less time for personal care like eating.

It shall be noted that, due to the observational nature of the original research, it is not possible to deduce any causal relationship. Usage of time is only one possible variable that can be associated with the stated satisfaction level about using own time. Other factors might include, for example, a prevalence of assertive kind of personalities among Finns.

Also, in order to verify whether these differences are statistically relevant, inference methods would need to be used. In order to use those methods, it would be necessary to get more statistical data about the variability of the distributions.

4. Conclusions¶

This study uses data about the average time used in different kind of activities by Finns living in private households. A comparison with similar data from Germany has been done.

Finns who state that they are more satisfied than Germans about the usage of their time also work more, travel less for leisure or domestic work and spend less time for personal care like eating.

In both Countries women spend more time in domestic work (which includes also maintenance and reparation), compared to men. They have less time for work and study related activities and less free time.

The time used for working and studying has decreased over time in Finland. In recent years we also notice an increase in free time. The share of domestic work has increased for men whereas has decreased for women over the years.

It shall be noted that, due to the observational nature of the original research, it is not possible to deduce any causal relationship.

Also, in order to verify whether these differences are statistically relevant, inference methods would need to be used. In order to use those methods, it would be necessary to get more statistical data about the variability of the distributions.
